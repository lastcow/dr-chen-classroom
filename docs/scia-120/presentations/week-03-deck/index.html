3.1 Defining Social Engineering

Social engineering in a security context refers to any technique that uses psychological manipulation — rather than technical exploitation — to gain unauthorized access to systems, data, or physical spaces.

The social engineer's targets are people, not machines.From the Week 03 reading material

1. Reciprocity

Core explanation

If an attacker performs a small kindness — helps you with a task, provides useful information — you feel a subconscious debt.

Classroom interpretation

That debt makes you more likely to comply with a subsequent request, even if it is inappropriate or security violating.

2. Commitment and Consistency

Core explanation

Once a person commits to a position or action, they are strongly motivated to remain consistent with that commitment.

Classroom interpretation

Attackers exploit this by first obtaining small, innocuous commitments and then escalating.

6. Scarcity

A

Main idea

People place higher value on things that are scarce and respond urgently to the prospect of missing out.

B

Security relevance

Creating artificial urgency ("I need this done in the next 10 minutes or the system will go down") bypasses deliberate thinking and pushes targets toward compliance without reflection.

Phishing

Core explanation

Phishing is the most prevalent form of social engineering attack.

Classroom interpretation

The attacker sends fraudulent emails designed to appear as legitimate communications from trusted sources — banks, technology companies, government agencies, or the target's own employer.

Spear Phishing

Core explanation

Spear phishing is a targeted variant of phishing in which the attacker customizes the message for a specific individual or organization.

Classroom interpretation

Using information gathered from social media, corporate websites, press releases, or prior reconnaissance, the attacker crafts a message that is highly credible to the specific target.
